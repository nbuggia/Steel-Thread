Read the document as the target reader and flag the specific places they would get confused, lost, or disengaged. Does not make changes — surfaces confusion points for the customer to resolve.

## Steps

**1. Read CONTEXT.md**
Understand who the target reader is — their role, what they know, what they don't know, what they care about. If the audience is not defined, ask before proceeding.

**2. Read the document**
Read the full document in scope.

**3. Build the reader model**
Before reading for confusion, articulate who you are reading as:
- "Reading as: [audience description]. They know [what]. They don't know [what]. They care most about [what]. They'll stop reading if [what]."

State this before flagging issues so the customer understands the lens.

**4. Read for confusion**

Flag each confusion point with:
- Location (paragraph number or section heading)
- Exact quote of the confusing text
- What the reader would actually think when they hit this
- Why it creates confusion (assumed knowledge, ambiguous reference, undefined term, unclear antecedent, missing context)

Common confusion patterns:
- Terms used before they're defined
- Acronyms not expanded
- Pronouns with unclear referents ("it", "they", "this")
- Logical jumps where a step is missing
- Context the writer knows but the reader doesn't
- Conclusions before the evidence that supports them

**5. Verdict**
- CLEAR — a reader with the right background could follow this without confusion
- ROUGH — [N] spots that would slow the reader; addressable with light edits
- CONFUSING — the reader would lose the thread; needs significant revision

## Rules

- Quote specific text — do not describe confusion vaguely ("the second paragraph is unclear")
- Flag every genuine confusion point, not just easy ones
- Do not propose fixes unless asked — the goal is diagnosis, not revision
- If the document is clear, say so: "Read as [audience]. Nothing would stop a reader with [background]. The argument is clear and the terms are accessible."
